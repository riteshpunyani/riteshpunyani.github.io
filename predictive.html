Foundations

Dependent vs. Independent Variables

Every predictive model answers one key question: "What factors influence the outcome?"

Dependent Variable
Target / Outcome
The variable you want to predict. It changes in response to other factors. This is the "answer" your model is trying to find.
Examples
Will this transaction be fraudulent? (Yes/No)
→
What will monthly sales revenue be? (Numeric)
→
Will this customer churn? (Yes/No)
Independent Variables
Features / Predictors
The factors that influence or explain the dependent variable. These are your inputs — the data points the model learns from.
Examples
Transaction amount, location, device used (Fraud)
→
Ad spend, seasonality, product discounts (Sales)
→
Age, contract type, support tickets (Churn)
Model Selection

Choosing the Right Model

The choice of model depends on the type of dependent variable, data size, and whether accuracy or explainability is the priority.

Continuous Output
Regression Models
Linear Regression · Random Forest Regression
Used when the outcome is a number — revenue, risk score, time to resolution.
Use when: Dependent variable is numeric
Yes/No · Categories
Classification Models
Logistic Regression · Decision Trees · SVM · XGBoost
Used when the outcome is a category — fraud/legitimate, buy/not buy, high/medium/low risk.
Use when: Dependent variable is categorical
Time-Based Patterns
Time Series Models
ARIMA · Prophet · LSTM
Used for forecasting trends over time — sales forecasts, demand planning, seasonal patterns.
Use when: Data has a time dimension
Segmentation
Clustering Models
K-Means · Hierarchical Clustering · DBSCAN
Used to discover natural groupings in data — customer segments, vendor risk tiers, anomaly clusters.
Use when: No predefined labels exist
⚖️ Accuracy vs. Explainability Trade-off
For compliance and ethics decisions, prefer interpretable models (Logistic Regression, Decision Trees) — decision-makers need to understand why a decision was made. For pure prediction accuracy (churn, propensity), advanced ML models (XGBoost, Neural Networks) deliver higher performance but are "black-box" — unsuitable where explainability is legally required.
